How Da Vinci's Notebooks
Changed the World

Imagine finding a locked box containing the private thoughts, sketches, and observations of the greatest mind of the Renaissance. That is, in essence, what Leonardo's notebooks are — and their rediscovery has shaped science, art, and philosophy for five centuries.

Leonardo kept notebooks obsessively throughout his adult life. He wrote in mirror script — right to left, with the letters reversed — a habit that may have been a form of code, or simply a comfortable way for a left-handed man to write without smearing ink. The notebooks contain everything: shopping lists alongside aerodynamic calculations, recipes next to anatomical drawings, architectural plans sketched beside notes on the nature of light.

There are approximately 7,200 pages of Leonardo's notebooks surviving today, distributed across institutions in Milan, Paris, Madrid, Windsor, and Seattle. But this represents only a fraction of what he produced; biographers estimate that he filled perhaps 13,000 pages over his lifetime. After his death, the notebooks passed to his student Francesco Melzi, and after Melzi's death they were scattered, lost, stolen, and only gradually recovered over the following centuries.

The impact of their rediscovery has been profound. His anatomical drawings, rediscovered in the 18th century, advanced medical understanding considerably. His engineering sketches — of bridges, hydraulic machines, and military devices — have been built and tested by modern engineers, many of whom confirm they were entirely functional. His observations on geology and hydrology anticipated theories that would not be formalised until the 19th century.

Perhaps most importantly, Leonardo's notebooks changed how we think about creativity itself. They showed that the greatest breakthroughs come from the willingness to question everything, to observe without prejudice, and to record without shame. In an age of digital overload, there is something quietly revolutionary about a man who simply sat, looked, and wrote down what he saw — with extraordinary honesty and extraordinary care.

His notebooks are not historical artefacts. They are an invitation — to slow down, to look more carefully, and to ask better questions.

"Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication."
— Leonardo da Vinci
